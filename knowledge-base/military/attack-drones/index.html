Attack FPV Drones

The war in Ukraine has fundamentally changed modern warfare. Cheap FPV drones costing $400-600 are destroying tanks, armored vehicles, and artillery positions worth millions. This is the most significant shift in warfare since the introduction of the machine gun.

The Paradigm Shift

$500 vs $5,000,000

A standard FPV attack drone costs roughly $400-600 to produce. It can destroy a T-72 tank ($1-5M), a BMP fighting vehicle ($500K), or an artillery system ($1-3M). The cost asymmetry is staggering — it's cheaper to build 10,000 drones than to replace a single tank battalion. This has completely disrupted traditional military economics.

Before FPV drones, anti-tank missiles like the Javelin cost $175,000 each. The Switchblade 300 loitering munition costs $6,000. FPV attack drones offer comparable capability at 1-5% of the cost. They can be produced in garages by volunteers, with components sourced from Chinese e-commerce platforms.

How Attack Drones Work

The System

  • Operator: Sits in a concealed position, often 5-15km from target
  • Goggles: FPV goggles provide real-time video feed from the drone
  • Radio: ELRS or similar long-range RC link for control
  • Video: Analog or digital video transmitter (analog preferred for cost)
  • Warhead: RPG-7 round, PG-7VL grenade, or improvised explosive (0.5-2kg)
  • Mission: Fly to target, impact and detonate — one-way trip

The operator flies the drone like an FPV racer — using the video feed for precise targeting. They guide the drone into armored vehicles, trenches, supply trucks, or infantry positions. The drone detonates on impact, carrying a shaped-charge warhead capable of penetrating armor.

Ukraine Production & Usage

Scale of Operations (2024-2025)

  • Ukraine aims to produce 1+ million FPV drones in 2025
  • Multiple volunteer organizations and military units mass-produce drones
  • Key manufacturers: Ukrainian volunteer networks, government programs
  • Training programs established for drone operators — weeks, not months
  • Drones are considered expendable — like artillery shells
  • Both sides use FPV drones extensively — Russia also scaling production

Fiber-Optic Guided Drones

A critical innovation: fiber-optic guided FPV drones carry a thin fiber cable (like fishing line) that unspools during flight. This provides a jam-proof communication link.

Why Fiber-Optic?

  • Immune to electronic jamming — No radio signal to disrupt
  • Extended range — 10-40+ km depending on cable weight
  • Reliable video — No interference, no signal loss
  • Can operate in GPS-denied areas — No RF signature for enemy to detect

Fiber-Optic Drone Pioneers

Both Ukraine and Russia have deployed fiber-optic guided FPV drones in combat. Ukraine's "Wild Hornet" and various Russian units have demonstrated these systems at ranges exceeding 20km. The technology is rapidly evolving, with thinner cables and longer spools being developed.

🔥 Fiber-Optic Guided Drones (2026)

One of the most significant developments in 2025-2026 is the rise of fiber-optic guided FPV drones. Connected to the operator by a thin fiber-optic cable, they are completely immune to electronic jamming.

Why Fiber-Optic? As RF jamming becomes widespread, fiber-optic drones offer a counter. The fiber carries both video and control signals. No RF = nothing to jam. Range: 5-20km.

Recent Developments (March 2026)

  • Dual-Control Drones: Ukraine testing drones with BOTH fiber-optic AND radio links. If one fails, the other takes over. Extra cost: just $57 per drone.
  • Air-to-Air Kill: Ukrainian fiber-optic FPV drone shot down a Russian Ka-52 helicopter near Pokrovsk
  • Ground Robot Launch: Ratel H UGV upgraded to launch fiber-optic FPV drones from forward positions
